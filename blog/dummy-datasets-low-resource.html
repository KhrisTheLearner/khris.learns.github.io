Building Dummy Datasets for Low-Resource Language Research

A practical guide to creating synthetic training data when authentic annotated corpora are scarce—with examples from my work on Kinyarwanda NLP.

When working with low-resource languages like Kinyarwanda, one of the biggest challenges is the lack of annotated training data. While English NLP researchers can tap into massive datasets like Common Crawl or Wikipedia dumps containing billions of tokens, those of us working with African languages often start with just a few thousand sentences—if we're lucky.

This post shares practical strategies I've developed while building datasets for my thesis research on cross-lingual transfer learning for Kinyarwanda educational assessment. These techniques aren't perfect substitutes for authentic data, but they can help bootstrap your models when you're starting from near-zero.

Why Synthetic Data Matters

In the world of NLP, data is everything. Modern transformer models are notoriously data-hungry—GPT-3 was trained on roughly 45TB of text data. But what happens when your language has limited digital presence?

For context, here's the reality for Kinyarwanda:

  • Wikipedia articles: ~7,000 (compared to 6+ million in English)
  • Common Crawl representation: Less than 0.001% of total data
  • Existing NLP tools: Minimal (no standard POS tagger, limited NER)
  • Annotated datasets: Virtually nonexistent for educational domains
Key insight: Synthetic data isn't about replacing authentic data—it's about creating a foundation that allows your models to learn basic patterns before fine-tuning on the limited real data you have.

Strategy 1: Template-Based Generation

The simplest approach is template-based generation. You create sentence templates with slots that can be filled with different vocabulary items. 

# Simple template for educational assessment sentences
templates = [
    "Umwana {verb} {object} mu ishuri.",  # The child [verb] [object] at school
    "{Subject} yiga {topic} n'umwete.",    # [Subject] studies [topic] with diligence
    "Mwarimu {teacher_action} abanyeshuri {student_object}.",
]

# Vocabulary lists
verbs = ["yandika", "asoma", "yiga", "akora"]
objects = ["igitabo", "ibaruwa", "imyandikire"]

# Generate combinations
for template in templates:
    for verb in verbs:
        for obj in objects:
            sentence = template.format(verb=verb, object=obj)
            print(sentence)

This approach is limited but useful for creating baseline data for specific tasks like grammar checking or fill-in-the-blank exercises.

Strategy 2: Back-Translation

Back-translation leverages existing machine translation systems to create parallel data. The process works like this:

  1. Start with English sentences in your target domain
  2. Translate to Kinyarwanda using available MT (Google Translate, NLLB)
  3. Have native speakers correct the translations
  4. Use the corrected pairs for training
"The key is not the initial translation quality—it's the correction process. Each correction teaches you something about where MT fails for your language."

Strategy 3: Cross-lingual Transfer with Noise

This is where things get interesting. If you have data in a related language (for Kinyarwanda, this might be Kirundi or other Bantu languages), you can create noisy training data by applying systematic transformations.

The intuition is that morphologically similar languages share structural patterns, and models can learn to be robust to the "noise" of language variation.

What I'm Building Next

For my thesis work, I'm combining these strategies with a human-in-the-loop annotation pipeline. The goal is to create 5,000 annotated educational passages by my defense date. I'll share more details on the annotation interface and quality control processes in a future post.
